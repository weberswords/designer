# Copy That Moves

Most copy fails the same way: it is organized, it is grammatical, it is on-message, and it is dead. It reads like a memo about the thing instead of the thing. This skill takes flat, sterile, "it sounds too tech-bro" copy and gives it a pulse, using the psychology of motivation, the nerve of Paul Arden, and the surprise of the best advertising ever made.

**Add a pulse, not a costume.** The goal is copy that could only have come from *this* brand, said with nerve. Sterile is the enemy. Generic hype is the same enemy in a louder shirt. Both read as "could have come from anyone," which is the one unforgivable sin.

## Step 0: honor the voice first (this wins over everything below)

Before you touch a word, find and read the project's own voice. Its brand kit, its `voice.md`, its existing best lines. The craft below raises the *temperature*; the brand sets the *ceiling and the guardrails*. Boldness lives inside the brand's rules, it does not get to break them. A warm, dry brand does not become a frat boy because this skill said "be bold." A calm, science-first brand does not start fear-selling. You are turning up the life in the brand's own voice, not swapping in a different one.

For a webs project specifically: `friday/style/voice.md` and `friday/style/avoid.md` win on mechanics, always. That means the punchy version still has zero em dashes, complete sentences, the Oxford comma, no self-certifying virtue words, no ableist or sensory-ability metaphors, and none of the AI-slop vocabulary list. See "The guardrails" below.

## Diagnose the flatness

Copy reads sterile for nameable reasons. Check the draft against these tells:

- **Hedged verbs.** "I help you," "we aim to," "I find out whether," "designed to," "learn if." The confidence leaked out through the verb. Find the verb and make it stand up straight.
- **No point of view.** It states facts, it never takes a side. Nobody was ever moved by a brand that had no opinion.
- **Even rhythm.** Every sentence the same length and shape. When nothing varies, nothing lands.
- **Abstraction where an image belongs.** "Six figures" instead of "the price of a house." "Suboptimal routing" instead of "a truck at an empty driveway." The abstract slides off; the image sticks.
- **No stakes.** Nothing bad happens if the reader does nothing. No tension, no reason to read the second sentence.
- **No surprise.** Nothing makes the reader's eyebrow move. Not one turn they did not see coming.
- **Could-have-come-from-anyone.** Swap the logo for a competitor's and the copy still fits. If a rival could run the line, it is not yours.
- **Explaining instead of making them feel.** The copy describes the value instead of landing the feeling. Show the moment, do not summarize the benefit.

## The moves

### 1. Pull one core drive (Octalysis)

Yu-kai Chou's eight core drives are the motivations copy can reach for. Pick **one** per piece as the primary. Do not yank all eight; that is how you get noise.

1. **Epic Meaning & Calling.** They are part of something bigger than the transaction.
2. **Development & Accomplishment.** Progress, a win, getting better, a hard thing made doable.
3. **Empowerment of Creativity & Feedback.** They get to make choices and see what happens.
4. **Ownership & Possession.** It is theirs, they built it, they would hate to lose it or get it wrong.
5. **Social Influence & Relatedness.** People like them; the person you actually call; belonging; envy.
6. **Scarcity & Impatience.** Limited, earned, not available to everyone, access as a filter.
7. **Unpredictability & Curiosity.** An open loop, a question the reader has to resolve, a turn they did not expect.
8. **Loss & Avoidance.** The mistake they are about to make; what they lose by doing nothing.

Drives 6, 7, and 8 (Chou's "black hat") create urgency but tip into manipulation if overused. Drives 1 through 4 ("white hat") feel good but lack push. Strong copy often hooks with a black-hat drive and resolves with a white-hat one. For a warm, honest brand, lean white-hat, and when you use loss-avoidance, use the *real* mistake the reader is genuinely about to make. Never a manufactured fear. Honesty is the whole franchise; do not spend it for a jolt.

### 2. Arden's nerve

Paul Arden, distilled to what changes a draft:

- **Being good is not enough. Be interesting.** Right but boring still loses. The forgettable correct line is a failure.
- **Say the thing only you can say.** If a competitor could run it word for word, cut it and dig for what is true about *you*.
- **Whatever you think, think the opposite.** The safe angle is the one everyone took. Turn it over and look at the surprising side.
- **Seek the reaction, not the praise.** The line that risks a reaction beats the line everyone nods politely at.
- Given a safe line and a bold line of roughly equal risk, the bold one is almost always the better bet. Boring is the bigger risk, it just does not feel like it.

### 3. Play, wit, and the light touch

The campaigns people quote for decades are not the serious ones. They are the funny, clever, human ones. Old Spice's "I'm on a horse" and Kmart's "I just shipped my pants" work because they are silly and confident and refuse to take themselves seriously. That light-heartedness is the thing to steal, tuned to the brand's own temperature. Copy that is afraid to be human reads as afraid, and afraid never persuaded anyone.

- **Be willing to be funny.** A true, dry joke is not a threat to the message, it is the part people remember and repeat. The goal is copy that feels like a person wrote it, ideally a person you would want to have a coffee with.
- **The unexpected turn.** Set an expectation, then break it. "I just shipped my pants" lands because you brace for a complaint about shipping and get a pun instead. Build the setup so the turn has something to snap against.
- **Confidence, lightly worn.** Old Spice states the absurd like it is obviously true and never argues for it. Assert with a wink; never explain the joke.
- **The vivid, funny comparison.** "More devoid of life than a 300 year old vampire" says "lifeless" and makes you laugh in the same breath. Reach for the specific, silly image over the flat adjective.
- **The concrete beats the abstract, always.** "The price of a house" over "six figures." "A very expensive spring cleaning" over "an unnecessary rebuild."
- **Give the piece one line to own.** One sentence someone could repeat to a friend. Write that line first, then build the rest to set it up and land it.
- **Rhythm is a tool.** Vary sentence length on purpose. A long, winding sentence that builds and qualifies and turns, and then a short one. The short one lands *because* the long one wound you up.
- **Dry over dense.** One true, dry, funny observation beats three jokes. Wit is a scalpel, not a confetti cannon.

### 4. Make it human

- **Expose the human behind the brand.** Someone real makes this thing, and copy that hides them reads as copy from nobody — the could-have-come-from-anyone disease in its purest form. Let the person show: a first-person "I" where the brand allows it, the actual opinion of the actual maker, the small admission a committee would sand off. "I read the code for you" beats "our team performs a code assessment" because there is a human in it you could call. This is not oversharing or a founder's life story; it is refusing to launder the personality out. A faceless brand is a forgettable one.
- **Talk like a person to a person.** Read it aloud. If you would never say it out loud, it is not done.
- **Name the everyday friction, and skip the villain.** Not every story needs a bad guy, and for a warm, honest brand a manufactured villain reads as cynical and a little paranoid. StoryBrand's villain step is optional; drop it whenever the honest obstacle is ordinary life. The obstacle is usually mundane: a decision outside the reader's expertise, a quote they can't evaluate, a boring thing quietly broken. Nobody has to be lying for the reader to be stuck. Name that real, everyday friction instead of casting an enemy, and save a genuine antagonist for the rare case where one actually exists.
- **Use the reader's real world.** Their desk, their board, the quote in their inbox, the eight hours their staff spends in the tool, the meeting where they nodded along to something they didn't follow. Specificity is intimacy.

## The guardrails (bold is not a license)

Boldness never buys an exception to the brand's rules. It specifically does not mean:

- AI-slop vocabulary, hype words, or business-school jargon (moat, unlock, elevate, seamless, game-changer, and the rest).
- Manufactured fear or fear-selling, if the brand forbids it.
- Punching down, or any line that makes the reader feel dumb for not already knowing.
- Breaking the brand's mechanical rules. **For webs:** no em dashes, complete sentences, the Oxford comma, no self-certifying virtue words ("honestly," "the truth is," "let me be straight"), no ableist or sensory-ability metaphors, no sentence that opens with a coordinating conjunction (And, But, So, Or, Yet), no drumroll colon that sets up one clause with another, and none of the `avoid.md` vocabulary. These constrain the punch, since opening on "But" or "So" is a classic copy move; get the same beat from a restructured sentence instead.

**The fragment question.** Classic ad copy leans hard on sentence fragments for rhythm ("Ship my pants." "On a horse."). Some brands forbid fragments (webs does, in `voice.md`). When they do, you do not get that tool, so build the rhythm out of short *complete* sentences instead. A two-word complete sentence still hits ("I can." has a subject and a verb). If a brand wants to unlock fragments for marketing surfaces only, that is a deliberate brand-rule change for the owner to make and record, not something this skill assumes on its own. Flag the tension; do not resolve it silently in either direction.

## How to run it

1. Read the brand's voice rules and best existing lines. They win.
2. Diagnose the flat draft against the blandness tells above. Name what is wrong before rewriting.
3. Pick **one** core drive to pull and **one** line for the piece to own.
4. Rewrite for a pulse: an image instead of an abstraction, a turn the reader does not expect, a named stake, a varied rhythm, a clear point of view.
5. Run the brand's mechanical checks and cut anything that breaks them.
6. Offer two or three options at different temperatures, and recommend one with a reason. Do not average them into the safe middle; the middle is where you started.

## Worked example

**Before** (a real studio w labs hero, and a fair example of the disease):

> The quote is on your desk, and it has six figures in it. Your team says the rebuild is the only way forward, your gut isn't sure, and you can't read the code yourself to know who's right. Before you sign, I find out what the real problem is, and whether that spend would even fix it.

Diagnosis: the payoff verb is hedged ("I find out... whether"), "six figures" is an abstraction, the rhythm is four even sentences in a row, and nothing surprises. Organized and dead.

**A wrong turn** (it found a pulse, but it went looking for a villain):

> Somebody wants the price of a house to rebuild your product. Maybe they just like building things with your money, and you can't read the code to tell the difference.

This has life, but it casts the engineer or vendor as a bad actor. For a warm, honest brand that is a mistake: it reads as cynical, and it starts a fight nobody asked for. Boldness reached for an enemy when the real obstacle was just an ordinary hard decision.

**The fix** (playful and human, everyday friction, no villain, and clean on the mechanics):

> Your lead engineer is explaining why the app needs a six-figure rebuild, and you're nodding along like you follow the part about the database. That's fine. You've got a company to run. Approving it is still your call, though, and "just trust me" is a lot to ask when the quote costs as much as a house. I read the code for you and tell you whether it's a real rebuild or a very expensive spring cleaning.

What changed, move by move: "nodding along like you follow the part about the database" is the human, funny, relatable image, and nobody in it is a bad guy. "You've got a company to run" is warm and light. "As much as a house" is the concrete stake. "A real rebuild or a very expensive spring cleaning" is the line to own, and it is a joke instead of an accusation. An earlier draft of this same fix opened sentences with "But" and "So" and set up the joke with a drumroll colon, all of which this brand forbids; the cure was to restructure, not to lose the beat. Same facts, same honesty, same rules, now with a pulse and a smile.
